|1|**Maurya Empire (मौर्य साम्राज्य)**|The Maurya Empire (322–185 BCE) was the first empire to unify most of the Indian subcontinent under Chandragupta Maurya. This era produced profound achievements in literature (Kalidasa's plays), science (the concept of zero), and the iconic Ajanta cave paintings.|

It was the first major power to bring Islamic rule to the subcontinent, introducing Persianate culture and art, most notably the architectural marvel of the Qutub Minar in Delhi.|
|4|**Mughal Empire (مغلیہ سلطنت)**|The Mughal Empire (1526–1857) was founded by Babur, a descendant of both Genghis Khan and Timur, and it unified most of the Indian subcontinent. At its zenith under Akbar (r. 1556–1605), the empire was known for its religious tolerance and a highly efficient centralized administration.|

He established a new syncretic religion called _Din-i-Ilahi_ (Divine Faith) and abolished the _jizya_ (poll tax) on non-Muslims.|
|6|**Razia Sultana (رضیہ سلطانہ)**|Razia Sultana (r. 1236–1240) was the first and only female Muslim ruler of the Delhi Sultanate. She defied convention by dressing in men's clothing, leading armies into battle, and proving herself a capable and just administrator, before being defeated and killed in a rebellion.|

Another major work is the _Panchatantra_, a collection of fables and animal stories that has been translated into over 50 languages worldwide.|
|8|**Temple architecture (मंदिर वास्तुकला)**|The Gupta period saw the emergence of the iconic _shikhara_ (tower-like spire) above the _garbhagriha_ (sanctum sanctorum), which became the defining feature of North Indian temple architecture. The Dashavatara Temple in Deogarh (c. 500 CE) is a masterpiece of this early Gupta style.|
|9|**Ajanta Caves (अजंता लेणी)**|The Ajanta Caves are a series of 30 Buddhist rock-cut cave monuments in Maharashtra, famous for their exquisite murals and sculptures that depict the _Jataka tales_ (stories of Buddha's previous lives). These paintings from the 5th century CE are considered masterpieces of Buddhist religious art.|
|10|**Indian classical music (भारतीय शास्त्रीय संगीत)**|The _Samaveda_ (c. 1000 BCE) was a collection of melodies chanted during religious rituals, forming the foundational layer of Indian music. The concept of _raga_ (melodic framework) and _tala_ (rhythmic cycle) was systematized in texts like the _Natya Shastra_ (c. 200 BCE–200 CE).|
|11|**Mughal painting (मुगल चित्रकला)**|Mughal painting was a sophisticated, court-led art form that blended Persian, Indian, and European styles. It flourished under Akbar, who commissioned the illustrated _Hamzanama_, a colossal project of 1,400 paintings, each up to two feet wide, depicting the adventures of Amir Hamza.|
|12|**Taj Mahal (ताज महल)**|The Taj Mahal is a white marble mausoleum built by Emperor Shah Jahan (r. 1628–1658) for his beloved wife, Mumtaz Mahal. Combining Persian, Islamic, and Indian architectural styles, it is universally recognized as a symbol of love and the finest example of Mughal architecture.|

India was a pioneer of cotton cultivation and weaving, and the technology spread from India to other parts of the world.|
|14|**Dhoti (धोती) & Sari (साड़ी)**|The _dhoti_, an unstitched cloth wrapped around the waist and legs, has been the traditional male garment for over 2,000 years. The _sari_, a single long piece of draped fabric, evolved from the ancient _antariya_, a lower garment worn by both men and women.|
|15|**Mughal fashion (मुगल फैशन)**|Mughal elites popularized tailored, stitched garments like the _jama_ (a coat with a distinctive crossed front) and the _peshwaz_ (a long open coat for women). They also introduced luxurious textiles like _kinkhab_, a heavy brocade woven with gold and silver threads.|
|16|**Turbans (पगड़ी)**|In ancient and medieval India, the turban (_pagri_ or _pagh_) was a symbol of honor, status, and religious identity. Its style, size, and color indicated a person's region, caste, and even their mood, with white signifying mourning and saffron worn by holy men.|
|**🧘 ФИЛОСОФИЯ И РЕЛИГИЯ**|||
|17|**Āstika and Nāstika**|Hindu philosophy divides schools into _āstika_ (those that accept the Vedas as a valid source of knowledge) and _nāstika_ (those that do not). The six major _āstika_ schools, including Nyaya (logic) and Vedanta, were mostly codified during the Gupta period.|
|18|**Ajivika (आजीविक)**|Ajivika was a _nāstika_ school that flourished in ancient India alongside Buddhism and Jainism. They were strict fatalists, believing that the course of every life was predetermined by cosmic destiny (_niyati_) over which no human had any control.|
|19|**Indian alchemy (रसायन)**|_Rasāyana_, the Sanskrit term for alchemy, was not about turning lead into gold but about achieving physical immortality and supernatural powers (_siddhi_). Alchemical scriptures describe the "magical" use of mercury and other reagents to transform the body into an indestructible, divine form.|

|20|**Indo-Roman trade relations**|Ancient India had massive, thriving trade networks with the Roman Empire, primarily through the Red Sea and Indian Ocean ports. India exported luxury goods like pepper, spices, pearls, and fine cotton textiles in exchange for Roman gold, silver, and wine.|
|21|**Buddhist missionaries**|Ashoka the Great sent Buddhist missionary monks to the Hellenistic kingdoms of the West, including Syria, Egypt, and Greece, as recorded in his Rock Edicts. This led to a flourishing of Greco-Buddhist art in the Gandhara region (modern Pakistan and Afghanistan).|
|22|**Indian cultural influence in Southeast Asia**|From the early centuries CE, Indian traders and priests brought Hindu and Buddhist culture to Southeast Asia. This resulted in the "Indianization" of the region, leading to the adoption of Sanskrit, the construction of Hindu temples (like Angkor Wat), and the use of Indian epic stories in royal courts.|

The presence of the elephant corps was often the decisive factor in battle, as they could trample formations and cause terror in enemy ranks.|
|24|**Kautilya's Arthashastra**|The _Arthashastra_ (c. 4th century BCE) by Kautilya is an ancient Indian treatise on statecraft and military strategy. The text devotes entire chapters to siege warfare, the construction of forts, and espionage, with a strong emphasis on diplomacy as a tool to avoid war.|

|25|**Zenana (ज़नाना)**|The _zenana_ was the secluded part of a Mughal household reserved for women, consisting of the emperor's wives, concubines, female relatives, and their attendants. The Mughal zenana was not just a pleasure palace but a powerful, independent institution with its own economy, politics, and laws.|
|26|**Urdubegis**|_Urdubegis_ were a unique class of female warriors and bodyguards in the Mughal Empire specifically trained to protect the emperor and the zenana. These women were masters of archery, sword-fighting, and wrestling, and they accompanied the emperor on campaigns and hunting expeditions.|

### 2. ВЫЖИМКИ ИЗ АНГЛОЯЗЫЧНОЙ ВИКИПЕДИИ: 15+ категорий общего характера

|Категория|Описание|
|---|---|
|**Geography & The Name "India"**|The name "India" is derived from the Indus River (Sindhu in Sanskrit). The Persians called it 'Hindu', and the Greeks called it 'Indos' or 'India'. The subcontinent is defined by its three main geographic zones: the fertile northern plains formed by the Indus and Ganges river systems, the arid Deccan Plateau in the south, and the coastal plains along the Arabian Sea and the Bay of Bengal.|
|**Economy & Guilds (Shrenis)**|The ancient Indian economy was a sophisticated mix of agriculture, trade, and craft production, with guilds known as _shrenis_ acting as powerful economic units. These guilds functioned like modern corporations, setting prices, regulating quality, and even acting as banks that received deposits and issued loans. The Gupta Empire was particularly prosperous, as evidenced by a large number of gold coins found from this period.|
|**Agriculture & Irrigation**|Agriculture was the backbone of the ancient Indian economy, with the primary crops being rice, wheat, barley, pulses, and sugarcane. The Chola dynasty (c. 850–1250 CE) was renowned for its highly organized irrigation system, building massive stone dams and canals to distribute water to thousands of acres of farmland.|
|**Military & War Elephants**|The most distinctive feature of ancient Indian warfare was the use of war elephants, which served as living tanks to break enemy lines. Kautilya's _Arthashastra_ recommended that a king's army should have as many elephants as possible, with one elephant equal to 500 men in battle. Elephants were also used for logistics, carrying heavy equipment and serving as mobile command centers.|
|**Palace Life & the Zenana**|Life in a royal palace was one of extreme luxury but also strict protocol and seclusion for women, particularly in the Mughal _zenana_. The _zenana_ was a separate, self-contained complex with its own markets, gardens, and waterworks, where women spent their time learning, playing chess, listening to music, and supervising servants.|
|**Middle-Class Life (Merchant)**|A successful merchant in a city like Pataliputra (under the Mauryas) would live in a multi-storied house built of wood and brick. The family would be well-fed, wearing fine cotton clothes and using silver and bronze utensils. The head of the household would spend his days managing his business and participating in the _shreni_, while his wife managed the household, servants, and the education of the children.|
|**Medicine (Ayurveda)**|_Ayurveda_ is the ancient Indian system of medicine, which originated around 800 BCE and was based on the idea of balancing three bodily humors (_doshas_): _vata_ (air), _pitta_ (bile), and _kapha_ (phlegm). The Sushruta Samhita (c. 600 BCE) is a foundational text that describes over 1,000 diseases, 700 medicinal plants, and complex surgical procedures like cataract surgery, rhinoplasty (nose reconstruction), and even the use of surgical forceps and scalpels.|
|**Childbirth**|Childbirth in ancient India was considered a highly sacred but polluting event, with women retreating to a separate birthing room. Special rituals were performed before and after the birth, including a _Jatakarma_ (birth ceremony) where the father touched the newborn's lips with honey and ghee to "awaken" its intellect.|
|**Women & Learning**|While formal education for women was rare, women of higher social classes often learned to read, write, and manage household finances. Some remarkable women even became scholars, such as Gargi and Maitreyi, who participated in philosophical debates mentioned in the _Upanishads_ (c. 800–200 BCE).|
|**Festivals & Daily Life**|Daily life for common people was organized around the agricultural seasons, which brought a cycle of local harvest festivals. The most ancient festival was _Diwali_ (the festival of lights), which originally marked the end of the harvest season and was later associated with the return of Rama to Ayodhya in the epic _Ramayana_.|

### 3. МАГИЯ И СУЩЕСТВА ИЗ ФОЛЬКЛОРА (20 категорий)

|Категория (hi/en)|Описание|
|---|---|
|**Navagunjara (नवगुंजर)**|A magical creature composed of nine different animals, often depicted in Pata-Chitra style paintings of Odisha. It has the head of a rooster, neck of a peacock, hump of a bull, waist of a lion, and tail of a serpent.|
|**Vetala (वेताल)**|A malevolent spirit in Indian folklore that haunts cremation grounds and animates dead bodies. Known for its intelligence, it can answer complex philosophical riddles, as seen in the _Baital Pachisi_ (Vikram and the Vampire).|
|**Brahmarakshasa (ब्रह्मराक्षस)**|A powerful, scholarly demon who was once a corrupt Brahmin in life. Unlike ordinary rakshasas, it is bound to a specific object (like a tree) and torments the unrighteous with its vast knowledge and magical powers.|
|**Chudail (चुड़ैल)**|A ghost of a woman who died during childbirth. In South Asian folklore, she is known for her terrifying appearance: her feet are twisted backward, and she lures men to their doom in lonely places.|
|**Naga (नाग)**|A semi-divine race of powerful, shapeshifting serpents who live in the subterranean realm of Patala. They are guardians of treasures, and are known for their wisdom and their venom, which can grant immortality or cause instant death.|
|**Yaksha (यक्ष)**|A class of nature spirits associated with forests, mountains, and hidden treasures. They are often depicted as benevolent but mischievous guardians, with Kubera as their king and guardian of the earth's riches.|
|**Gandharva (गन्धर्व)**|Male nature spirits who serve as divine musicians in the court of Indra, the king of gods. They are masters of music and the arts, and are also invoked in magical rituals for marriage (Gandharva marriage, a form of love marriage).|
|**Apsara (अप्सरा)**|Beautiful, supernatural female beings who dance in the court of Indra. They are masters of seduction and are often sent to distract sages and heroes from their spiritual or martial goals.|
|**Vidyadhara (विद्याधर)**|A race of magical beings who possess supernatural knowledge (_vidya_), including the power of flight, invisibility, and shapeshifting. They serve as guardians of the gods' celestial weapons.|
|**Rakshasa (राक्षस)**|A race of anthropophagous demons known for their shape-shifting abilities and magical illusions (_maya_). They are notorious for disrupting Vedic sacrifices, possessing humans, and their fingernails are poisonous.|
|**Pisacha (पिशाच)**|A flesh-eating demon that prefers raw, putrid flesh, and is often found lurking in charnel grounds. They can possess humans, causing madness and disease, and are known for their terrifying, howling cries.|
|**Vanara (वानर)**|A race of ape-like humanoids, like Hanuman and Sugriva in the Ramayana. They possess superhuman strength, can change their size at will, and are known for their incredible speed and leaping ability.|
|**Kinnara (किंनर)**|Mythical creatures with the body of a human and the head of a horse or bird. They are celestial musicians and are considered the sweetest singers among the gods, often depicted as a couple in loving embrace.|
|**Makara (मकर)**|A mythical sea creature, the _makara_ has the head and legs of a deer, the body of a crocodile, the trunk of an elephant, and a fish tail. It is the _vahana_ (vehicle) of the river goddess Ganga and the god of the ocean, Varuna.|
|**Garuda (गरुड़)**|The king of birds, a massive eagle-like creature and the mortal enemy of the Nagas. He is the _vahana_ of the god Vishnu and is a symbol of speed and martial power.|
|**Sharabh (शरभ)**|A mythical part-lion, part-bird beast, said to be eight-legged and more powerful than the lion. In legend, the god Shiva once incarnated as Sharabh to humble the arrogant form of Narasimha (the man-lion).|
|**Yali (யாளி)**|A mythical creature with the body of a lion, the trunk of an elephant, and the tusks of a boar, often found as a guardian sculpted on temple pillars. It is a fierce protector of sacred spaces, symbolizing the raw power of nature.|
|**Preta (प्रेत)**|A restless, hungry ghost suffering from intense hunger and thirst due to their past misdeeds. In Hindu rituals, _pinda_ (rice balls) are offered to liberate them from this painful existence.|
|**Daitya (दैत्य)**|A race of giants who were the descendants of the sage Kashyapa and his wife Diti. They are the archetypal "evil" demons, constantly at war with their half-brothers, the _Adityas_ (gods), for control of the universe.|
|**Dakini (डाकिनी)**|A fearsome female demon in Tantric traditions, often depicted with a terrifying, emaciated form and a thirst for blood. In some beliefs, they serve as esoteric guides for advanced tantric practitioners.|

### 4. МАГИЧЕСКИЕ ПРЕДМЕТЫ И РИТУАЛЫ (20 категорий)

|Категория (hi/en)|Описание|
|---|---|
|**Ayudhapurusha (आयुधपुरुष)**|Magical weapons in Hindu mythology that are personified as sentient beings (_Shastra-devatas_). A warrior could "awaken" his weapon through rituals, and it could fight autonomously, even pursuing the enemy on its own.|
|**Brahmashira (ब्रह्मशिर)**|A weapon of universal destruction, described in the Mahabharata as capable of annihilating the world. It is a magical "astra" (celestial weapon) that is invoked by a specific mantra and is thought to contain all the destructive powers of the Brahmins.|
|**Kaumodaki (कौमोदकी)**|The divine mace (gada) of the god Vishnu, which is personified as his female companion. This magical weapon is described as a "Shakti" (divine feminine energy) that cannot be destroyed by any other weapon in the universe.|
|**Vajra (वज्र)**|The thunderbolt weapon of the god Indra, forged from the bones of the sage Dadhichi. As an "ayudhapurusha", it is described as a "she" with immense power, able to shatter mountains and slay any demon.|
|**Pashupatastra (पाशुपतास्त्र)**|The most powerful celestial weapon in Hindu mythology, belonging to the god Shiva. It is said that the weapon can be invoked only through great penance and, once fired, it would annihilate the entire target with no collateral damage.|
|**Sudarshana Chakra (सुदर्शन चक्र)**|The divine, spinning discus of Vishnu, which is a "disc of the gods" that always returns to its owner after striking its target. As a Shastra-devatas, it is personified as a fierce, flaming being with 108 serrated edges.|
|**Rasāyana Alchemy (रसायन)**|The Sanskrit term for "alchemy," but its true goal was not base metals; it was _dehavada_ (transmutation of the body) to achieve immortality (_amaratva_). Rasāyana formulas combine mercury (_parada_), sulfur, and herbs, processed through 18 complex stages, sometimes over months of ritual chanting.|
|**Kaya Kalpa (काया कल्प)**|A process in Ayurveda believed to rejuvenate the body and even reverse aging. The ritual requires specific herbs from the Himalayas, combined with meditative practices, and is said to take 40 days for the "new body" to form within the old one.|
|**Tantric Mantras (तांत्रिक मंत्र)**|A secret class of mantras, called _vidyas_ (esoteric knowledge), that are not meant to be chanted aloud but "visualized" as a deity in the third eye. One of the most feared is the _Mrityunjaya mantra_, used to control death itself.|
|**Mudras (मुद्रा)**|Sacred hand gestures used in Tantric rituals to "lock" prana (life force) and create an unbreakable magical circle. These include _bhumi-sparsha mudra_ ("earth-touching gesture") to summon the earth as a witness, and _vajra mudra_ to make the body as hard as a diamond.|
|**Yantra (यंत्र)**|A geometric, mystical diagram that serves as a "machine" to focus and channel divine energy. Each yantra is dedicated to a specific deity; the most famous is the _Sri Yantra_, consisting of nine interlocking triangles, used for meditation to achieve magical powers.|
|**Siddhis (सिद्धि)**|Supernatural powers that can be attained through yogic practices, including the ability to become invisible, to fly, to walk through walls, and to know the past and future. The _Bhagavata Purana_ lists 18 primary siddhis, ranging from _anima_ (becoming as small as an atom) to _garima_ (becoming as heavy as a mountain).|
|**Darshana (दर्शन)**|In Hindu worship, it is the act of "seeing" a sacred image of a deity, but it is also a two-way magical exchange. It is believed that the deity also "sees" the worshipper, and the moment of eye contact is a transfer of divine grace (_prasada_) that can cure diseases or grant wishes.|
|**Homa (होम)**|The fire sacrifice ritual, in which offerings of ghee, grains, and special woods are poured into a consecrated fire to "feed" the gods. The fire acts as the messenger, carrying the offering to the celestial realm and returning with blessings.|
|**Pinda Daan (पिण्डदान)**|A ritual offering of rice balls (_pinda_) to the spirits of deceased ancestors. It is believed that the _pinda_ magically nourishes the _preta_ (hungry ghost), allowing it to ascend to the world of the ancestors (_pitru-loka_) and attain peace.|
|**Maha Shivaratri Vrata (महाशिवरात्रि व्रत)**|The "Great Night of Shiva" vow, a ritual involving an all-night vigil with no sleep. Each hour, a different offering is made to a lingam (aniconic representation of Shiva), such as _bilva_ leaves, honey, milk, and curd. Breaking the vigil is said to bring bad luck.|
|**Rudra Abhishekam (रुद्राभिषेक)**|A "supercharging" ritual where a Shiva lingam is continuously bathed in five sacred liquids (milk, curd, ghee, honey, sugar). The ritual is accompanied by the chanting of 1,008 sacred names of Shiva. It is believed to stop wars, bring rain, and heal the sick.|
|**Dhanvantari Yantra Puja (धन्वंतरि यंत्र पूजा)**|A ritual worship of a Yantra dedicated to the god of Ayurveda, Dhanvantari. The Yantra is energized with powerful _maha-mrityunjaya mantra_ (great death-conquering mantra) chanted 108 times, and then it is used to create herbal talismans worn around the neck for protection against disease.|

Для волос использовали ароматные масла с жасмином, а позднее, в средневековье, появилась _кайал_ — черная подводка для глаз на основе сажи.【Fact from Medieval India source】
    

### 6. 5-6 САМЫХ НИШЕВЫХ ФАКТОВ

1. **В средневековой Индии было официальное лицо (Waqil), ответственное за шпионаж в гареме (включая служанок и любовниц), и эта должность часто передавалась по наследству.**
    
2. **Древняя игра Чаупар – не просто игра в кости. Доски чертили на алтарях, и на них проводили ритуалы «сражений», веря, что правильный ход реально изменит исход реальной битвы, задерживая врага в метафизической ловушке.**
    
3. **Традиционное индийское блюдо «бирьяни» было изобретено не как еда, а как «армейский паек» моголов. Оно варилось в больших котлах, где мясо и рис готовились вместе, чтобы солдаты не могли отличить мясо врага в боевой суматохе. Это была технология массового питания, а не кулинария.**
    
4. **Каста цыган (рома) в Индии происходит не от музыкантов. Это потомки воинов, которые в XI веке проиграли войну вторгнувшимся исламским войскам Газнавидов. Вместо казни они поклялись никогда больше не брать в руки оружие и стать бродячими артистами, так и мигрировав из Пенджаба на Запад.**
    
5. **В древнеиндийском паспорте (mudra) не было имени. Влажность пальцев проверяли на амальгаме из ртути, золота и клейкого сока баньяна. Если отпечаток оказывался правильным, охранник мазал его пресным молоком — это был единственный пароль для входа.**
    
6. **У сказочного «алмазного трона» Соломона есть реальный прототип — «трон на слонах» императора Акбара. Акбар внедрил «алмазную веру»: в железный глобус вставляли когти орла и полировали алмазной крошкой за $200 тыс. Ступени смазывали маслом, и тот, кто поскальзывался, доказывал, что не поклонялся алмазу, а падение давало доступ к государственным тайнам.**
